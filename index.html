01

About This Report

This report has two purposes: first, to articulate a working model of the mechanisms through which the Breathe for Change Master’s program produces impact — from product design through user engagement to intended outcomes; and second, to use that model to identify and prioritize opportunities for impact measurement. The model is the analytical foundation. The measurement priorities follow directly from it.

The analysis draws on three sources: a discovery conversation with BFC’s founding team on May 22, 2026; publicly available information at breatheforchange.com; and Cobalt Collective’s framework for evaluating impact measurement readiness in education, health, and workforce technology. It is a first-pass working draft, not a final assessment. We expect it to sharpen as BFC shares more about its program design, internal data, and strategic priorities.

Our aim is not to evaluate BFC’s impact, which is substantial and growing, but to help BFC’s leadership see clearly where the causal chain is well understood, where it is assumed but untested, and where the most important measurement investments should go — in what order, and why.

Evidence Landscape

Before identifying measurement opportunities, it is worth cataloging the evidence BFC already has. This inventory shapes the measurement roadmap: some priorities build on existing assets, others fill gaps, and some require starting from scratch. Understanding the current landscape prevents duplicating work and clarifies where the real investment is needed.

BFC has accumulated a meaningful body of evidence across its 11-year history — but nearly all of it was generated for its short-form training programs, not the M.S.Ed. As BFC shifts its strategic focus fully to higher education, the central challenge is that its most rigorous evidence describes a different product than the one it is now selling.

04

Impact Measurement Opportunities

A central finding of this analysis is that BFC faces two distinct measurement challenges that require different strategies and run on different timescales. They are related, but they are not the same problem. The first challenge involves building impact measurement into operations to generate business-relevant evidence of student engagement, personal transformation, pedagogical practice, and career outcomes.

The second is a measurement development challenge: the constructs at the heart of BFC’s model — the five Human Intelligences — do not yet have validated measures. The instruments BFC currently uses (the Perceived Stress Scale, the Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire, and similar) measure constructs that are meaningfully adjacent to the five intelligences but are not the same thing. Wellbeing, mindfulness, and stress reduction are real and important outcomes. But they are not measures of somatic intelligence, relational intelligence, or universal intelligence as BFC defines them. Building a validated Human Intelligence Scale is a contribution BFC is uniquely positioned to make and would create a valuable IP moat.

We therefore organize the measurement roadmap into two parallel tracks:

Track 1 — Measure What You Can Now. Use existing instruments and build low-lift data collection infrastructure to build a credible, defensible evidence base in the near term. This track produces data that serves enrollment marketing, district tuition support, accreditors, and funders — today.

Track 2 — Build the Measurement Infrastructure. Develop validated measures of the five Human Intelligences. This track is the long-term scientific investment that makes everything in Track 1 more meaningful, and that serves as a meaningful differentiator.

These independent tracks run in parallel — Track 2 begins now, at whatever pace resources allow, because the development timeline for validated psychometric instruments is long. The priorities within each track are sequenced; the tracks themselves are concurrent.

Track 1

Measure What You Can Now

The priorities in this track are sequenced: each one is more meaningful if the prior one is in place. But all four can begin within the next 12 months.

1a Program Engagement Quality — The Behavioral Foundation Now Start Here ▼

This is the right place to start because it requires no new data collection — only systematization of what BFC already has. Completion rates, cohort participation quality, and capstone rigor are the leading indicators of everything downstream. They are also product improvement data: understanding what drives deeper engagement directly informs how the program is designed.

What to measure

  • Program completion and retention rates by cohort
  • Cohort participation and peer engagement quality (survey-based)
  • Capstone quality — rubric scores, external or faculty evaluation
  • Real-classroom practice integration (self-report in Phase 2)

This data is almost entirely internal and available now. The opportunity is to treat it as impact data, not just operational data.

1b Personal Transformation — Pre/Post the Master’s Program Now Near-Term Evidence ▼

The most direct question individual educators ask when considering BFC’s program is: what will this do for me personally? BFC’s existing evidence — from prior short-form training programs — partially answers this using validated instruments like the Perceived Stress Scale and the Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire. But that evidence does not transfer to the M.S.Ed., which is a fundamentally different intervention in scope, duration, and design.

These instruments measure constructs that are adjacent to the five Human Intelligences — wellbeing, mindfulness, stress, burnout — but they are not direct measures of the intelligences themselves. They are the best available proxies while the direct measures are being developed (see Track 2). Used transparently, they are valuable and credible. Used as substitutes for the real thing, they leave BFC’s core claims unsupported.

What to measure

  • Wellbeing, stress, and burnout (Perceived Stress Scale, Maslach Burnout Inventory)
  • Mindfulness and self-awareness (Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire or equivalent)
  • Sense of purpose and professional identity (validated instruments exist for both)
  • Emotional regulation and relational skills (adjacent proxies for emotional and relational intelligence)

Collect these metrics pre-enrollment and post-graduation for every cohort, beginning immediately. They will serve enrollment marketing, accreditation, and funder reporting. They will also anchor validation in Track 2 by establishing the baseline against which new instruments can be calibrated.

1c Pedagogical Practice Change — What Educators Do Differently 6–12 months Outcome Evidence ▼

This is the mechanism connecting personal transformation to professional outcomes — and an important link in the evidence chain when it comes to district willingness to provide tuition support, higher-education accreditation, and research credibility.

Clark raised the CLASS observation framework (Pianta, UVA) as one existing option, measuring emotional support, classroom organization, and instructional support. BFC may want something more aligned to the five Human Intelligences, which extend beyond what CLASS covers — particularly in the somatic and universal dimensions. For now, the right move is to begin with what exists and adapt it, while the Track 2 work develops instruments tailored to BFC’s model.

What to measure

  • Self-reported changes in instructional practice (custom instrument, post-graduation)
  • Observer-rated classroom practice for a subsample (adapted CLASS or similar)
  • Student-reported classroom experience (for those with access to their own students)

Recommendation: begin with a well-designed self-report instrument for all graduates. Pilot an observation component with a small willing subsample. BFC’s 600+ capstone projects are an underutilized data source — systematic analysis of those could begin building this evidence without a separate study.

1d Career and Professional Outcomes — The Long-Term Pitch 12–24 months High Value, Lower Urgency ▼

BFC has abundant anecdotal evidence of graduates advancing into leadership roles — SEL coordinators, assistant principals, principals, superintendents. Ilana cited Teach For America’s alumni tracking model as the right analogy. Converting anecdote to systematic data here would be relatively low-cost and high-impact for enrollment marketing — it is the most compelling evidence that the BFC credential changes careers, not just classrooms.

What to measure

  • Role changes within 1–3 years post-graduation (annual alumni survey)
  • Salary and compensation changes (self-report)
  • Leadership and professional opportunities (speaking, coordinating, publishing)
  • Sustained engagement in the profession — retention as an educator

Priority 1d not because it matters less, but because it is distal and attribution is harder — which is why getting the proximal measurement right first is essential.

Track 2

Build the Measurement Infrastructure

Track 2 is the investment that makes BFC’s impact model scientifically defensible and positions BFC as a contributor to the field rather than a consumer of it. The two priorities in this track are explicitly sequenced: the theory of change comes first because it is the specification document on which the scale development depends. Both begin now — the theory of change immediately, the scale development once the constructs are defined with sufficient precision.

2a Theory of Change — The Specification Document 12–24 months Start Here in Track 2 ▼

The theory of change is the specification document for everything that follows — in Track 2 and in the curriculum itself. It answers three questions with rigor: What exactly is each of the five Human Intelligences? What is the learning science underlying this theory of intelligence? How does the program develop each one — what are the active mechanisms? And what behavioral and professional outcomes does that development produce? Answering these questions is foundational to writing valid items for a construct that has not been defined precisely.

The theory of change also functions as a curriculum audit tool. If somatic intelligence requires a particular kind of embodied practice to develop, the Phase 1 curriculum should contain it — and the theory of change makes that testable. BFC’s 600+ capstone action research projects are a rich input to this work: they represent practitioner evidence of what the program actually produces, which should inform and test the theory as it is developed.

What this involves

  • A formal written theory of change: each intelligence → developmental practice → behavioral outcome → distal impact
  • A literature review grounding each causal link in existing research
  • A research agenda identifying the highest-priority remaining empirical questions
  • A synthesis of capstone project findings as a practitioner evidence base
  • Possibly: a peer-reviewed publication in an education or applied psychology journal

This is what transforms BFC from a growing program into a field-defining institution.

2b Develop the Human Intelligence Scale 24–48 months Anchored in Theory of Change ▼

With the theory of change in hand, BFC has the construct definitions needed to build something no one else has built: a validated measure of the five Human Intelligences. The theory of change (Priority 2a) makes this possible by specifying what each intelligence means precisely enough to operationalize into scale items.

The development of a psychometric instrument is a multi-stage process. Each stage builds on the theory of change and on the adjacent-construct data being collected in Track 1, which provides calibration benchmarks.

What this involves

  • Construct definition: for each of the five intelligences, specify what it means to have more or less of it — observable, behavioral definitions that can be operationalized into items
  • Item development: write candidate questionnaire items that capture each construct, drawing on existing adjacent scales (PSS, FFMQ, etc.) and on BFC’s own curriculum and competency model
  • Expert review and cognitive testing: review items with the faculty team and test with a small sample of current students for clarity and face validity
  • Pilot administration: administer the candidate scale to a cohort pre and post, alongside existing adjacent measures for calibration
  • Psychometric analysis: examine reliability, factor structure, and convergent/discriminant validity against the adjacent measures
  • Refinement and publication: publish the validated scale and make it available to the field

BFC’s faculty team — including a UCSF neuroscientist, a clinical psychologist with expertise in trauma and belonging, a somatic practices expert, and others — is exactly the right group to define these constructs rigorously. This is not a task to contract out. It is the intellectual core of BFC’s model, and it should be owned internally.

Clark’s lab developed web-based performance assessments of SEL competencies funded by the Institute of Education Sciences. The development process and lessons learned from that work are directly relevant here — and available to BFC through Cobalt Collective.

05

Recommended Next Steps

The two-track structure above implies a specific set of near-term actions. Track 1 items can begin immediately. Track 2 items should begin in parallel, even if at a slower pace, because the development timelines are long.

Track 1 — Near-term
1
Engagement data audit. Map what completion, cohort participation, and capstone data BFC currently collects operationally, and identify the gaps. A half-day conversation with the academic team would be sufficient to scope this.
2
Instrument selection for personal transformation measurement. Identify a short battery of validated instruments covering wellbeing, burnout, mindfulness, purpose, and relational skills — assessing what BFC already uses and what can be adapted. Administer pre-enrollment and post-graduation to the next cohort.
3
Stakeholder mapping for research use. Identify the specific stakeholders for whom impact data will matter most in the next 12–24 months — district partners, accreditors, funders, prospective students — and what standard of evidence each requires.
4
Capstone synthesis scoping. Assess the feasibility of a systematic review of BFC’s 600+ capstone projects as a practitioner evidence base. A graduate student or research assistant could conduct an initial scan.
Track 2 — Begin now, longer horizon
5
Theory of change drafting. Begin outlining the formal theory of change document, starting with behavioral, observable definitions of each of the five Human Intelligences. Convene BFC’s faculty team — with Cobalt Collective facilitating — for a focused session.
6
Curriculum audit against the theory. Once draft construct definitions exist, review the three-phase curriculum against them: does each phase contain the mechanisms the theory says are needed to develop each intelligence? High-value, low-cost.
7
Scale development scoping. Once the theory of change is sufficiently specified, begin scoping the Human Intelligence Scale: review existing adjacent instruments for items that can be adapted, identify gaps that require original item development.
