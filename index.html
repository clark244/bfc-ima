Key Findings

The core mechanism is defined but not yet formalized
BFC's model rests on how engagement develops the five Human Intelligences and translates into improved teaching. It is grounded in faculty expertise but not yet written as a formal theory of change — the most important and least-specified link in the chain.
The strongest evidence describes a different product
Nearly all of BFC's rigorous evidence — the ESSA Level II/III studies, the COVID-era pre/post data — was generated for its short-form training, not the M.S.Ed. As BFC shifts to higher education, its best evidence describes a product it is moving away from.
The richest M.S.Ed.-specific asset is sitting unused
The 600+ capstone action research projects are the most promising existing data source about the M.S.Ed. itself, but have never been systematically analyzed. Synthesizing them is a near-term way to build product-specific evidence without a new study.
Two tracks, run in parallel, on different timescales
Use existing instruments now to build a defensible near-term evidence base (Track 1), while developing validated measures of the five Human Intelligences as a longer-term differentiator (Track 2). Track 2 begins now because psychometric development is slow.

Evidence Landscape

Before identifying measurement opportunities, it is worth cataloging the evidence BFC already has. This inventory shapes the roadmap: some priorities build on existing assets, others fill gaps, and some require starting from scratch.

BFC has accumulated a meaningful body of evidence across its 11-year history — but nearly all of it was generated for its short-form training programs, not the M.S.Ed. As BFC shifts its focus fully to higher education, the central challenge is that its most rigorous evidence describes a different product than the one it is now selling.

Two patterns emerge. First, BFC's existing evidence is almost entirely about a product it is moving away from — the short-form training; the M.S.Ed. has no dedicated evidence base yet, despite 600+ graduates. Second, the most promising M.S.Ed.-specific data source — the capstone projects — is sitting unused. Both observations directly inform the measurement priorities that follow.

Measurement Opportunities

A central finding of this analysis is that BFC faces two distinct measurement challenges that require different strategies and run on different timescales. The first is to build impact measurement into operations — generating business-relevant evidence of engagement, personal transformation, pedagogical practice, and career outcomes.

The second is a measurement-development challenge: the constructs at the heart of BFC's model — the five Human Intelligences — do not yet have validated measures. The instruments BFC uses today (the Perceived Stress Scale, the Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire, and similar) capture constructs meaningfully adjacent to the five intelligences but not the same thing. Wellbeing, mindfulness, and stress reduction are real outcomes, but they are not measures of somatic, relational, or universal intelligence as BFC defines them. A validated Human Intelligence Scale would be a genuine IP moat.

We therefore organize the roadmap into two parallel tracks. Track 1 — Measure What You Can Now: use existing instruments and low-lift data collection to build a credible near-term evidence base that serves enrollment marketing, district tuition support, accreditors, and funders today. Track 2 — Build the Measurement Infrastructure: develop validated measures of the five Human Intelligences — the long-term scientific investment and differentiator. Track 2 begins now, because validated psychometric instruments take a long time to develop.

Track 1

Measure What You Can Now

The priorities in this track are sequenced — each is more meaningful if the prior one is in place — but all four can begin within the next 12 months.

Measurement Focus Which audience each priority serves Student Recruitment District Tuition Support Accreditation & Funders Field Leadership
1a Program Engagement Quality — The Behavioral Foundation Now ○ ○ ● – ▼
Start Here

This is the right place to start because it requires no new data collection — only systematizing what BFC already has. Completion rates, cohort participation quality, and capstone rigor are the leading indicators of everything downstream. They are also product-improvement data: understanding what drives deeper engagement directly informs how the program is designed.

What to measure

  • Program completion and retention rates by cohort
  • Cohort participation and peer-engagement quality (survey-based)
  • Capstone quality — rubric scores, faculty or external evaluation
  • Real-classroom practice integration (self-report in Phase 2)

This data is almost entirely internal and available now. The opportunity is to treat it as impact data, not just operational data.

1b Personal Transformation — Pre/Post the Master's Program Now ● ● ● – ▼
Near-Term Evidence

The most direct question individual educators ask is: what will this do for me personally? BFC's existing evidence — from prior short-form training — partially answers this using validated instruments like the Perceived Stress Scale and the Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire. But that evidence does not transfer to the M.S.Ed., a fundamentally different intervention in scope, duration, and design.

These instruments measure constructs adjacent to the five Human Intelligences — wellbeing, mindfulness, stress, burnout — but they are not direct measures of the intelligences themselves. They are the best available proxies while the direct measures are developed (see Track 2). Used transparently, they are valuable and credible; used as substitutes for the real thing, they leave BFC's core claims unsupported.

What to measure

  • Wellbeing, stress, and burnout (Perceived Stress Scale, Maslach Burnout Inventory)
  • Mindfulness and self-awareness (Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire or equivalent)
  • Sense of purpose and professional identity (validated instruments exist)
  • Emotional regulation and relational skills (adjacent proxies)

Collect pre-enrollment and post-graduation for every cohort, starting immediately. This serves enrollment marketing, accreditation, and funder reporting — and anchors Track 2 by establishing baselines to calibrate new instruments against.

1c Pedagogical Practice Change — What Educators Do Differently 6–12 months ○ ● ● – ▼
Outcome Evidence

This is the mechanism connecting personal transformation to professional outcomes — an important link when it comes to district tuition support, higher-education accreditation, and research credibility.

The CLASS observation framework (Pianta, UVA) is one existing option, measuring emotional support, classroom organization, and instructional support. BFC may want something more aligned to the five Human Intelligences, which extend beyond what CLASS covers — particularly the somatic and universal dimensions. Begin with what exists and adapt it while Track 2 develops tailored instruments.

What to measure

  • Self-reported changes in instructional practice (custom instrument, post-graduation)
  • Observer-rated classroom practice for a subsample (adapted CLASS or similar)
  • Student-reported classroom experience (where graduates have their own students)

Begin with a well-designed self-report instrument for all graduates; pilot an observation component with a small willing subsample. The 600+ capstone projects are an underused source — systematic analysis could begin building this evidence without a separate study.

1d Career and Professional Outcomes — The Long-Term Pitch 12–24 months ● ○ ○ – ▼
High Value, Lower Urgency

BFC has abundant anecdotal evidence of graduates advancing into leadership — SEL coordinators, assistant principals, principals, superintendents. Teach For America's alumni-tracking model is the right analogy. Converting anecdote to systematic data here would be relatively low-cost and high-impact for enrollment marketing — the most compelling evidence that the credential changes careers, not just classrooms.

What to measure

  • Role changes within 1–3 years post-graduation (annual alumni survey)
  • Salary and compensation changes (self-report)
  • Leadership and professional opportunities (speaking, coordinating, publishing)
  • Sustained retention in the profession

Sequenced 1d not because it matters less, but because it is distal and attribution is harder — which is why getting the proximal measurement right first is essential.

Track 2

Build the Measurement Infrastructure

Track 2 makes BFC's impact model scientifically defensible and positions BFC as a contributor to the field rather than a consumer of it. The two priorities are sequenced: the theory of change comes first, because it is the specification document the scale development depends on. Both begin now — the theory of change immediately, scale development once constructs are defined precisely enough.

Measurement Focus Which audience each priority serves Student Recruitment District Tuition Support Accreditation & Funders Field Leadership
2a Theory of Change — The Specification Document 12–24 months ○ ○ ● ● ▼
Start Here in Track 2

The theory of change is the specification document for everything that follows — in Track 2 and in the curriculum itself. It answers, with rigor: What exactly is each of the five Human Intelligences? What learning science underlies them? How does the program develop each one — what are the active mechanisms — and what behavioral and professional outcomes does that development produce?

It also functions as a curriculum audit tool: if somatic intelligence requires a particular embodied practice to develop, the Phase 1 curriculum should contain it — and the theory of change makes that testable. BFC's 600+ capstone projects are a rich input, representing practitioner evidence of what the program actually produces.

What this involves

  • A formal written theory of change: each intelligence → developmental practice → behavioral outcome → distal impact
  • A literature review grounding each causal link
  • A research agenda identifying the highest-priority open questions
  • A synthesis of capstone findings as a practitioner evidence base
  • Possibly a peer-reviewed publication

This is what transforms BFC from a growing program into a field-defining institution.

2b Develop the Human Intelligence Scale 24–48 months ○ ○ ● ● ▼
Anchored in Theory of Change

With the theory of change in hand, BFC has the construct definitions needed to build something no one else has: a validated measure of the five Human Intelligences. Priority 2a makes this possible by specifying what each intelligence means precisely enough to operationalize into items.

Developing a psychometric instrument is a multi-stage process. Each stage builds on the theory of change and on the adjacent-construct data collected in Track 1, which provides calibration benchmarks.

What this involves

  • Construct definition: observable, behavioral definitions of each intelligence
  • Item development, drawing on adjacent scales (PSS, FFMQ) and BFC's competency model
  • Expert review and cognitive testing with faculty and a small student sample
  • Pilot administration to a cohort pre/post alongside adjacent measures
  • Psychometric analysis: reliability, factor structure, convergent/discriminant validity
  • Refinement and publication of the validated scale

BFC's faculty — including a UCSF neuroscientist, a clinical psychologist expert in trauma and belonging, and a somatic-practices expert — is exactly the right group to define these constructs. This is not a task to contract out; it is the intellectual core of BFC's model and should be owned internally.

Cobalt Collective's team developed web-based performance assessments of SEL competencies funded by the Institute of Education Sciences; that development process and its lessons are directly relevant and available to BFC.

Recommended Next Steps

The two-track structure implies a specific set of near-term actions. Track 1 items can begin immediately; Track 2 items should begin in parallel, even at a slower pace, because the development timelines are long.

Track 1 — Measure What You Can Now

1
Engagement data audit. Map what completion, cohort-participation, and capstone data BFC currently collects, and identify the gaps. A half-day conversation with the academic team would scope this.
2
Instrument selection for personal-transformation measurement. Identify a short battery of validated instruments covering wellbeing, burnout, mindfulness, purpose, and relational skills; assess what BFC already uses and can adapt. Administer pre-enrollment and post-graduation to the next cohort.
3
Stakeholder mapping for research use. Identify the stakeholders for whom impact data will matter most in the next 12–24 months — district partners, accreditors, funders, prospective students — and the standard of evidence each requires.
4
Capstone synthesis scoping. Assess the feasibility of a systematic review of the 600+ capstone projects as a practitioner evidence base. A graduate student or research assistant could conduct an initial scan.

Track 2 — Build the Measurement Infrastructure

5
Theory of change drafting. Begin outlining the formal theory of change, starting with behavioral, observable definitions of each of the five Human Intelligences. Convene BFC's faculty — with Cobalt Collective facilitating — for a focused session.
6
Curriculum audit against the theory. Once draft construct definitions exist, review the three-phase curriculum against them: does each phase contain the mechanisms the theory says are needed to develop each intelligence? High-value, low-cost.
7
Scale development scoping. Once the theory of change is sufficiently specified, begin scoping the Human Intelligence Scale: review adjacent instruments for items that can be adapted, and identify gaps requiring original item development.

About This Report

This report has two purposes: first, to articulate a working model of the mechanisms through which the Breathe for Change Master's program produces impact — from product design through user engagement to intended outcomes; and second, to use that model to identify and prioritize opportunities for impact measurement.

  1. Articulate a working model of how the BFC Master's program produces impact — from product design through engagement to outcomes.
  2. Use that model to identify and prioritize opportunities for impact measurement.

The model is the analytical foundation; the measurement priorities follow directly from it. Our aim is not to evaluate BFC's impact, which is substantial and growing, but to help leadership see where the causal chain is well understood, where it is assumed but untested, and where the most important measurement investments should go — in what order, and why.
